She also has two copies of a variant (SOD2) that reduces her cells' ability to neutralize oxidative stress — essentially, her "internal rust protection" isn't as strong as average. Neither of these is a disease or a diagnosis, but together they mean her body could use some extra support right now, especially while healing from surgery. The good news: both are manageable with the right supplements (see the Supplement Stack tab). Primary actionable variants
MTHFR — C677T (p.Ala222Val) Heterozygous (TC) rs1801133 · CADD 25.5
Think of MTHFR as the enzyme responsible for turning the folate you eat (from food or supplements) into the version your body can actually use. Kaitlyn has one copy of a variant that makes this enzyme run at about 60–65% efficiency instead of 100%. That's not a crisis, but it means her body is less effective at a process called methylation — which affects everything from how well she detoxifies to how her brain makes feel-good chemicals, to how efficiently her cells repair themselves. It's particularly relevant right now because surgery puts extra demand on all of those systems. The practical fix is straightforward: take the pre-converted form of folate (methylfolate) instead of regular folic acid, so her body doesn't have to do the conversion at all.
Action: take methylfolate (not regular folic acid) + methylcobalamin B12 · ask your doctor to run a homocysteine blood test to see if this is causing any real-world impact
SOD2 — Val16Ala Homozygous (AA) rs4880 · CADD 13.03
Every cell in your body produces a small amount of "exhaust" as a natural byproduct of making energy — similar to how a car engine produces emissions. SOD2 is the enzyme that cleans up that exhaust before it damages the cell. Kaitlyn has two copies of a variant that makes this cleanup enzyme less effective at getting where it needs to go inside the cell. The result is that her cells accumulate slightly more oxidative "wear and tear" than average, especially during demanding periods — like recovering from major surgery, which is one of the highest-oxidative-stress events the body goes through. The supplement CoQ10 (in its active form, ubiquinol) helps compensate for this directly. This isn't dangerous on its own, but it does mean her body needs a little extra antioxidant support to heal well.
Action: ubiquinol CoQ10 (active form — look for "ubiquinol" not "ubiquinone") · NAC supplement · eat colorful fruits and vegetables · prioritize sleep during recovery — that's when cellular repair happens
GSTP1 — Ile105Val Variant present (heterozygous) rs1695 · CADD 0.035
The liver has a two-step system for processing and removing toxins and waste products from the body. GSTP1 is one of the key enzymes in step two — it packages up harmful substances so they can be safely flushed out. Kaitlyn has a variant that makes this enzyme slightly less efficient. Combined with her SOD2 variant (which means more cellular waste is being produced), her liver's cleanup system is being asked to do more with slightly less capacity. This isn't a problem in everyday life, but it does mean she should be mindful about not overloading her system with unnecessary medications or alcohol during recovery, and that eating foods like broccoli, kale, and cabbage can actually help activate this pathway.
Action: NAC supplement supports the detox pathway · eat cruciferous vegetables regularly (broccoli, kale, Brussels sprouts) · avoid unnecessary medications during recovery · limit alcohol
ADD1 — Gly460Trp Heterozygous (AG) rs4961 · CADD 31
This gene affects how the kidneys handle salt. Most people's kidneys let excess sodium pass through in urine — Kaitlyn's kidneys are genetically inclined to hold onto more of it than average. That matters because when your body retains extra sodium, it retains extra water with it, which raises blood pressure. She doesn't have high blood pressure now, and this variant alone won't cause it — but it does mean she's more sensitive to salty diets than the average person. If she were to eat a consistently high-sodium diet over time, her blood pressure would likely respond more than most people's would. Easy to manage: watch the salt, eat more potassium-rich foods (which help offset sodium's effects), and keep an eye on blood pressure readings.
Action: go easy on salt and processed/fast foods · eat potassium-rich foods (bananas, avocado, sweet potato, leafy greens) · check blood pressure monthly — flag anything consistently above 125/80
APOE — e2/e3 Heterozygous (TC) rs7412 · CADD 25.1
APOE is a gene involved in how the body processes fats and cholesterol. There are three main versions — e2, e3, and e4. Most people have two copies of e3. Kaitlyn has one e2 and one e3, which is actually a favorable combination: the e2 version is associated with lower LDL ("bad") cholesterol and a lower risk of Alzheimer's disease compared to people who carry e4. The one thing to be aware of is that e2 carriers can sometimes see triglycerides (a type of fat in the blood) run a bit high if their diet is heavy in saturated fat. That's easily managed with diet and a simple blood test once a year. Overall this is a good-news genetic finding.
Favorable variant overall · get an annual cholesterol panel · keep an eye on triglycerides specifically · moderate red meat and fried foods
CFH — His402Tyr Heterozygous (TC) rs1061170
The retina at the back of your eye has a self-cleaning system that removes cellular debris as it builds up over time. CFH is a protein that helps regulate that process. Kaitlyn has one copy of a variant that makes this cleanup system less efficient, which over decades can lead to a buildup of deposits in the central part of the retina — the condition known as age-related macular degeneration (AMD), which affects sharp central vision. This is a long-term risk, not an immediate problem — AMD typically shows up after age 60. But it's worth knowing about now because there are straightforward things she can do to slow it down: a specific eye supplement (lutein and zeaxanthin), regular dilated eye exams so any early changes are caught, and avoiding smoking, which dramatically accelerates AMD in people with this variant.
Action: start lutein + zeaxanthin supplement now · get a dilated eye exam to establish a baseline · annual eye exams going forward · don't smoke — smoking is the single biggest accelerator of AMD risk with this variant

COMT is the enzyme that clears dopamine and adrenaline from the brain after they've done their job. Some people have a "slow" version of this gene, which means those chemicals linger longer — often associated with heightened pain sensitivity, anxiety, and stress reactivity. Kaitlyn has the normal, efficient version. In the context of her surgery, this is a meaningful positive: it suggests she'll have a more typical response to pain management and anesthesia, and that her stress response system is working as expected. Nothing to worry about here.

NQO1 is an enzyme that, among other things, helps recycle CoQ10 — a compound her supplement stack includes to support the SOD2 deficiency. Think of CoQ10 like a rechargeable battery inside cells. NQO1 is the charger. Some people have a variant that breaks the charger, meaning CoQ10 supplements don't work as well for them. Kaitlyn has the normal version — her "charger" works fine, which means the CoQ10 she takes will be recycled and used efficiently. Good news that makes her supplement stack more effective.
Favorable — CoQ10 supplementation will work as intended

Most people in the world lose the ability to comfortably digest dairy as they get older — that's lactose intolerance, and it's actually the genetic norm for humans. Kaitlyn has two copies of a gene variant that keeps her dairy-digesting enzyme (lactase) active for life. She can eat dairy without issue, which is good news nutritionally — especially now, when she needs adequate calcium and protein to support bone healing from her surgery.
No action needed — dairy is fine and actually helpful during bone recovery
