Primary actionable variants
MTHFR — C677T Homozygous (TT) rs1801133 · CADD 25.5
MTHFR is the enzyme that converts folate from food and supplements into the active form the body can actually use. Having two copies of this variant (homozygous TT) means that enzyme runs at roughly 30–35% of normal efficiency — more than twice as impaired as having just one copy. This affects a critical body process called methylation, which touches everything from how well cells repair themselves to how the brain makes serotonin and dopamine, to how efficiently the liver detoxifies waste products. For Kaitlyn specifically — recovering from major surgery right now — her body's repair and detox demands are at a peak while the underlying machinery is running significantly below capacity. The fix is well understood: take the pre-converted, active form of folate (methylfolate) so the body skips the broken conversion step entirely.
Action: methylfolate (L-5-MTHF, not regular folic acid) + methylcobalamin B12 + P5P B6 · get a homocysteine blood test — if elevated, confirms real-world methylation impact and guides supplement dosing · note: raw DNA confirmed she does NOT carry the A1298C variant — this is C677T homozygous only, not compound heterozygous
MTRR — Ile22Met Heterozygous (AG) rs1801394 · CADD 20.9
MTRR is a helper enzyme that keeps the body's B12-dependent methylation machinery running — think of it as the maintenance crew for the engine that MTHFR feeds into. This variant reduces how efficiently the body regenerates the active form of B12 used in the methylation cycle. Combined with homozygous MTHFR, these two variants compound each other — more methylation impairment than either alone. The protocol needs to address both the folate side (methylfolate) and the B12 side (methylcobalamin) to adequately compensate for both.
Action: reinforces the need for methylcobalamin B12 specifically — not cyanocobalamin · sublingual delivery is most effective · don't skip the B12 component of the methylation stack
COMT — Val158Met Homozygous (AA) rs4680 · CADD 13.15
COMT is the enzyme responsible for clearing dopamine and adrenaline from the brain and nervous system after they've done their job — think of it as the brake on stress chemicals. Kaitlyn has two copies of the slow version, meaning dopamine and stress hormones linger in her system longer than average. In practice this means heightened pain sensitivity, stronger emotional reactions, and more reactivity to stress than most people. This is clinically relevant right now: her nervous system is wired to amplify pain signals more than the average person, which means proactive pain management and stress reduction during recovery are particularly important. This is worth communicating directly to Dr. Savoie's team so post-op pain management is appropriately calibrated.
Action: tell Dr. Savoie's team she has slow COMT and heightened pain sensitivity · prioritize stress management and sleep during recovery · magnesium glycinate can support nervous system calm · avoid stimulants (caffeine, etc.) during recovery window
NQO1 — Pro187Ser Homozygous (AA) rs1800566 · CADD 24.8
NQO1 recycles CoQ10 — cells use CoQ10 like a rechargeable battery to make energy and neutralize cellular waste. NQO1 is the charger. Kaitlyn has two copies of a variant that breaks the charger — her body cannot efficiently recycle CoQ10 from its used-up form back to its active form. This has two practical consequences: first, her cells are more vulnerable to oxidative damage than her SOD2 result alone would suggest; second, standard CoQ10 supplements (ubiquinone) won't work well because her body can't recharge them. She specifically needs the pre-charged form — ubiquinol — which bypasses the NQO1 recycling step. Critically, she also has homozygous COQ2 (see below), meaning she can't build CoQ10 efficiently in the first place either. The combination of COQ2 (can't make it) + NQO1 (can't recycle it) + SOD2 (higher demand for it) represents a significant triple deficit in CoQ10 availability.
Action: must use ubiquinol CoQ10 (not ubiquinone) — this distinction matters more for her than almost anyone · 200–300mg daily minimum given triple COQ2/NQO1/SOD2 compounding · also relevant for cancer risk monitoring given NQO1's role in stabilizing the p53 tumor suppressor
SOD2 — Val16Ala Heterozygous (AG) rs4880 · CADD 13.03
SOD2 is the main antioxidant enzyme inside mitochondria — it neutralizes cellular "exhaust" before it damages surrounding cells. Kaitlyn has one copy of the variant (heterozygous, not two copies as previously noted — less severe). However, combined with her homozygous NQO1 variant (broken CoQ10 charger), her overall mitochondrial antioxidant capacity is still meaningfully reduced. Think of it as having both a weaker exhaust scrubber and a broken battery charger at the same time — the compounding effect matters, especially during surgery recovery when cell repair is at its peak.
